Why a Green Renewable Energy Network Linking The Meditteranean Sea Countries Is Needed Now.


By Laurie Shell | Postdoctoral Researcher at University of Portsmouth's Climate Action Research Center (CARC).

In this op-ed, Laurie Shell argues that a Meditteranean green energy network is a feasible and urgent idea for the region's future.

The Mediterranean region has enormous potential for green energy sources. Ranging from the hilly environs of Spain (the physically most mountainous country in Europe) to the sea resources inherent to the region to the sun-drenched (semi)deserts especially found in North Africa and the Levant.

Given the ongoing system-wide shifts in enegry provisioning, especially for Europe in the context of the Ukrainian War and deepening de-globalisation era of the 2020s

Unprecedented risks to the European energy system in this de-globalisation era with the Covid-19 supply shocks, followed by an inflationary cycle coming at the tail-end of secular central-bank-assured low-interest credit-first policies and, ultimately, the significant disruption to energy supplies from Europe's primary energy and raw minerals supplier, Russia, in the wake of sanctions and divestment in reaction to the illegal invasion and annexation of their Ukrainian neighbours.

With further deepening of the energy crisis in Europe in the context of inflation of energy prices and cuts to supply, the need for green energy has never been clearer or greater. The Mediterranean region is perfectly positioned to be the place for supplying Europe with reliable and less expensive energy.

The main barrier to achieving this is the lack of funding and ongoing focus on traditional resources. Funding is the more significant barrier and there can be two solutions to this issue. Private-public partnerships can encourage private enterprise to divest or shift from traditional dirty energy toward green energy projects, if they can be convinced of its profitability.

With the private sector being involved, there are greater chances of having successful projects being implemented- especially in the energy sector. According to a University of Newcastle study completed in 2017, public-private partnerships had an 37% greater return on investment(ROI) in contrast to similarly sized private-only or public-only projects.

With this model, there are also more reflexive outcomes, with greater engagemnet with entrepreneurial, civil society and other stakeholders in the process of planning and delivering energy projects that output sustainable outcomes not only in pure energy terms but so too with respect to community interests.

A University of Adelaide study from 2020 found that public-private partnerships in the transport and energy sectors provided much better quality of service(QoS) and especially was marked by greater anti-fragile and sustainable outcomes that kept providing local communities with employment, tax-based returns that were reinvested in community healthcare and education, as well as general outreach by the companies involved. The government's investments tended to create more trust in these projects from the communities.

There is thus a strong case for creating special tax and other favourable financial incentives targetting green energy projects in the medium term. In the shorter term, it would be useful to revisit large-scale energy projects such as the proposed Desertec project which proposed bringing solar power resources from the Middle East and North Africa region via pipelines over to Europe.

Alongside finance, the other key requirement is technological development, especially in the fields of energy conservation and transfer in order to now address the costs and feasibility of transferring captured green energy, especially solar power from the sunny Mediterranean region, to long-distances, in the North-West of Europe.

Time will tell if we can accomplish these massive changes in order to get the European economies no only to overcome its current challenges, but to even become a model for other parts of the world as we transition in energy sources for the future.

Laurie Shell.

 
 

The Meditteranean is ready for a tourism revolution.


By Frank LeBeuf | Business Research Analyst at Macquarie Partners, Paris.



In this op-ed, Frank LeBeuf argues that the Meditteranean region's traditionally vibrant tourist sector needs updating with new technologies that can provide a lasting experience beyond simply physical visits.

The Mediterranean region has always been a honeypot for tourism. Two of the three most popular countries for tourism by gross traffic are Spain and France, first and second. These countries manage to have a gigantic inflow of visitors from around the world despite their small size in contrast with the other countries that follow them on the tourist heavy-hitters list- the U.S., China and others.

Other Mediterranean countries like Italy, Turkey, Greece are ll heavy hitters with international tourism- particularly drawing northern European, Middle East and, increasingly, Asian visitors. Yet, despite the incredibly natural beauty, central location, and well of experience that these countries offer, they are not looking toward the future developments of the tourist industry.

This reality wwas particularly demonstated during the recent Covid-19 Pandemic. With the virus spreading around the world, some of the hardest-hit economic sectors and regions were the hospitality industry. A particularly infamous case was the Diamon Princess cruise liner, which found itself stuck in Japan in March 2020, but many other particular liners, and the industry in general sufferred unprecedented financial, reputational, and organisational shocks.

Tourist hotspots in the Mediterranean were perhaps not as drastically impacted as the cruise lines or Caribbean countries given that they have a more developed and diversified national, regional economic links. Yet, cities like Venice, Valetta, Ayia Napa, Palma and other key spots for Mediterranean tourism found themselves under unprecedented pressure. While the inital impact of these oft-overcrowded metropolises was celebrated, notably in Venice where, during the initial lockdowns, all kinds of wildlife was seen in the streets and canals, the longer-term loss of the tourism pillar in the economy and adjoined social services such as education and healthcare are now making themselves felt.



One particular solution for this crisis in tourism-heavy industries and regions is the prospect of integrated cyber-focussed delivery mechanisms to have a 'continuous delivery' of the tourism experience.
